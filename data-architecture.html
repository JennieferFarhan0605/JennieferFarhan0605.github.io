The Anatomy of a Healthcare Dataset

A dataset is not a spreadsheet. It is a set of decisions — about what matters, what to measure, and what story the numbers should be capable of telling. This page documents every choice made in constructing the operational data behind the Power BI healthcare dashboard — 20,130 rows of daily granularity across 7 tables and a full leap year.

The Why

A Case for Visible Medicine

Healthcare generates more operational data than almost any other industry — yet most of it lives in siloed EMRs, billing systems, and disconnected department trackers, never synthesised into a picture that allows leaders to act with clarity. The gap between what happens in a hospital and what management can actually see is, in many cases, a patient safety issue.

This dataset was built around a single pragmatic premise: if a department head sat down on the first Monday of each month, what twelve numbers would tell them whether their department was performing, struggling, or about to tip into crisis? Then: what would the CFO need alongside that? The clinical quality lead? The equipment manager running half-million-dollar MRI assets?

The most powerful thing you can do with healthcare data is not predict disease — it is make the ordinary, operational truth of a hospital legible enough that a manager can do something about it before it becomes extraordinary.

Design rationale — Jenniefer Farhan

Every field in this dataset was chosen because it is either a leading indicator (it tells you where you are going), a lagging indicator (it confirms what just happened), or a diagnostic bridge (it explains why a headline metric moved). No field exists merely because it was available.

Construction Method

How the Dataset Was Built

The data was generated programmatically in Python using openpyxl, with mathematical realism baked into every value. The goal was not random noise — it was structured verisimilitude: patterns that behave the way a real hospital would over a calendar year.

Step 01 — Domain Mapping
Identify the operational questions worth answering

Before writing a single line of Python, the six domains of hospital operations were mapped: patient flow, clinical quality, finance, physician performance, equipment assets, and workforce. Each domain became its own table. This mirrors the star-schema logic Power BI expects for efficient DAX calculations.

Step 02 — Baseline Research
Anchor every metric to published NHS / CMS benchmarks

Each base value — bed occupancy at 78%, 30-day readmission around 11.2%, ICU mortality near 8.5% — was calibrated to published ranges from CMS Hospital Compare, NHS England operational standards, and AHRQ quality indicators. The numbers are not real, but they are plausible.

Step 03 — Temporal Realism
Add seasonal variation and year-on-year growth curves

A sinusoidal seasonal function models the well-documented winter surge in emergency admissions and elective surgery troughs in summer. A compounding growth function layered on top creates the drift you would expect from a hospital incrementally improving its throughput across a year.

Step 04 — Controlled Noise
Apply department-specific jitter so no two rows are identical

Each cell is perturbed by ±3–15% random noise (tighter for stable metrics like mortality, wider for volatile ones like overtime hours). This prevents Power BI trend lines from being artificially smooth and makes anomaly detection actually meaningful.

Step 05 — Formatting & Validation
Colour-code, format, and verify zero formula errors

All sheets are formatted with the portfolio's mulberry and charcoal palette, alternating row shading by department for readability, and a frozen header row. LibreOffice recalculation was run as a final pass — confirmed zero formula errors across all 18 Excel formulas in the totals row.

build_dataset.py — temporal realism functions 
import math, random

# Seasonal amplitude: mirrors winter admissions surge, summer elective trough
def seasonal(month, amp=0.12):
    return 1 + amp * math.sin(2 * math.pi * (month - 3) / 12)

# Monotonic growth: models gradual operational improvement across the year
def grow(month, total=12, rate=0.08):
    return 1 + rate * (month - 1) / (total - 1)

# Controlled noise: ±pct perturbation so real patterns remain detectable
def jitter(val, pct=0.05):
    return val * (1 + random.uniform(-pct, +pct))

# Usage: winter bed occupancy for a high-volume department
month = 12  # December
occupancy = jitter(0.78 * seasonal(month), 0.03)
# → ~0.867  (realistic winter surge from baseline 78%)

Field Rationale

Why Each Metric Earns Its Column

The following documents the reasoning behind every key metric category. Fields without a clear decision-support purpose were excluded during scoping.

Clinical Quality & Safety
6 fields
30-Day Readmission Rate
The single most widely used indicator of discharge quality. A readmission within 30 days is the system admitting it sent someone home before they were ready. CMS uses it directly as a penalty trigger — so it belongs in any dashboard with real stakes.
% Rate
Hospital-Acquired Infection Rate
HAIs are the clearest signal of environmental and procedural discipline. They are preventable, they are costly, and a rising HAI rate gives infection control teams weeks of actionable lead time if it's visible. A dashboard without it is incomplete.
% Rate
Protocol Adherence Rate
Outcome metrics tell you what happened. Protocol adherence tells you whether the system was followed. It is the leading indicator to outcome's lagging. A department with low adherence and currently-good outcomes is an accident waiting to happen.
% Rate
Patient Reported Outcomes Score
PROs capture what clinicians and administrators cannot see from inside the system: whether the patient felt better. They are the primary measure of value from the payer's perspective in value-based care models and increasingly from regulators.
Score /10
In-Hospital Mortality Rate
Not included to sensationalise, but because risk-adjusted mortality is the ultimate lagging indicator for a clinical environment. Its slow, low-noise movement over time is precisely the kind of trend that gets invisible without monthly tracking.
% Rate
Complication Rate
An intermediate outcome sitting between adherence and mortality. It is the metric most sensitive to surgeon skill variation and equipment quality — making it invaluable for identifying which departments or physicians are driving anomalies.
% Rate
Operational Flow & Capacity
5 fields
Bed Occupancy Rate
The most fundamental capacity metric. Below 75% suggests waste; above 92% correlates with infection risk and delayed discharges. The 78% baseline here represents efficient steady-state, with seasonal peaks deliberately reaching 87–89% in winter months.
% Rate
Average Length of Stay
LOS is a proxy for everything: clinical acuity, discharge planning efficiency, bed pressure, and cost. A 0.5-day reduction in LOS across a 250-bed hospital is worth millions in freed capacity annually. It deserves monthly tracking at department level.
Days
OR Utilization Rate
Operating theatres are among the most expensive assets in a hospital. Underutilisation at 65% wastes capital; over 95% means no buffer for emergencies. The target band of 80–88% reflects clinical safety guidelines. A dashboard that misses this misses the hospital's engine room.
% Rate
Average Wait Time
Patient experience, regulatory compliance, and emergency triage quality all converge here. It is the field most visible to patients themselves, which makes it politically significant — and in emergency departments, clinically significant too.
Hours
Staff-Patient Ratio
Understaffing is the single most consistent predictor of adverse patient events across the published literature. This ratio, tracked monthly by department, becomes the connective tissue between the financial story (staff cost) and the clinical story (outcomes).
Ratio
Financial Performance
5 fields
EBITDA & EBITDA Margin
Revenue without context is meaningless. EBITDA by department separates the operationally efficient from the operationally subsidised. Oncology and ICU carry high margins in absolute terms but different margin percentages — the split reveals where true profitability lives.
$ / %
Insurance Denial Rate
A denial rate above 5% signals a revenue cycle problem that no amount of clinical excellence can compensate for. It is one of the least-reported but highest-impact financial metrics in US hospital operations, making it critical for any realistic financial dashboard.
% Rate
Days Sales Outstanding
Cash flow, not revenue, keeps hospitals solvent. DSO of 38 days (industry median) means over a month of earned revenue is sitting as receivables. A rising DSO combined with high denial rates is the early signature of a liquidity crisis in the making.
Days
Cost per Patient
Normalises cost data against volume so that high-cost departments are evaluated fairly. An ICU with £18,000 cost-per-patient is not necessarily inefficient — but one that is trending upward without an acuity explanation is a management issue.
$
Revenue by Stream (Inpatient / Outpatient / Procedure)
Aggregate revenue conceals strategic risk. A department where 70% of revenue is procedure-based is vulnerable to elective surgery cancellations in ways that an inpatient-heavy department is not. Stream decomposition enables scenario planning that aggregate numbers cannot.
$
Workforce Intelligence
4 fields
Vacancy Rate
A vacancy rate above 8% in a clinical department is not an HR issue — it is a patient safety issue. Unfilled positions mean existing staff work above safe ratios. Tracking it monthly at department level reveals which teams are structurally fragile.
% Rate
Agency Staff Usage
Agency staff cost 30–60% more per hour than substantive staff and carry continuity and familiarity risks. High agency usage alongside a high vacancy rate is the fingerprint of a retention problem masquerading as a recruitment problem.
Count
Turnover Rate
Nurse turnover costs an estimated $40,000–$65,000 per departure when accounting for recruitment, onboarding, and productivity loss. Monthly tracking at department level gives HR and department leads the granularity to intervene before experienced staff reach resignation.
% Rate
Training Hours
Included as a leading indicator for protocol adherence and complication rate — departments that invest in structured training consistently outperform on both. It also signals organisational health: a team under acute staffing pressure typically defunds training first.
Hours
Design Decisions

What Was Deliberately Left Out

Dataset design is as much about exclusion as inclusion. Several fields were considered and removed. The reasoning matters as much as the choices themselves.

✕

Individual Patient Records

Patient-level data would have enabled more sophisticated analysis but would require HIPAA/GDPR compliance architecture even for synthetic data — and would shift the dashboard from operational intelligence to clinical surveillance, a fundamentally different use case. The aggregate was the correct scope.

✕

Diagnostic Code Frequency (ICD-10)

Diagnosis distribution data would have added analytical richness but creates complexity in Power BI's data model that goes beyond what an operational dashboard warrants. Condition mix belongs in a clinical analytics tool, not an operational one. The procedure categories here serve as a sufficient proxy.

✓

Daily Granularity — Chosen Over Monthly Aggregates

Monthly aggregation was the initial default. It was rejected. Daily data at 20,130 rows exposes patterns that monthly summaries structurally conceal: weekend staffing drops, winter admission surges peaking mid-week, equipment downtime clustering around specific days. These are not noise — they are the operational truth of a hospital. Power BI handles this volume natively, and the Day of Week, Week No., and Quarter fields built into every table make it trivial to aggregate up when a monthly view is needed. The granularity flows downward; it cannot be recovered if you start at the top.

✕

External Benchmark Comparisons

CMS or NHS peer-group benchmarks were considered as additional columns. They were excluded because embedding them in the dataset would conflate the operational data layer with the analytical layer — a distinction Power BI is well-suited to handle at the report level via reference lines and targets, not in the source data.
