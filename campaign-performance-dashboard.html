Data Questions & Answers

Interrogating the numbers — what the data actually shows, why certain patterns appear, and what the metrics mean in practice.

Google / Organic drives 112,918 sessions but converts at roughly 0.41%. Google / CPC drives only 15,650 sessions yet converts at 1.28% — more than three times higher. The reason is intent. Organic captures a wide range of queries: informational, navigational, and transactional. Paid search can be targeted to commercial-intent keywords only, which self-selects for users actively in purchase mode. This volume-vs-efficiency divergence is the core argument for treating these channels as complementary rather than substitutable. High Organic volume builds brand surface area; CPC precision closes it.

The spike is Black Friday and Cyber Monday. Weekly purchases climb from 273 in Week 1 (Nov 1–7) to a peak of 833 in Week 6 (Nov 29 – Dec 5). The collapse is sharp: by Week 8, volume drops to 257, and by Week 9 to 170. The cause is the gift delivery window closing — most consumers purchasing for Christmas need to order by mid-December to guarantee delivery. After that hard deadline, purchase intent pauses rather than disappears. The January recovery (Week 12 reaches 373 purchases) confirms it: a temporal demand shift, not permanent destruction.

Because revenue follows sessions, and sessions are highly concentrated. Google / Organic and Direct together account for roughly 79% of total session volume — so they naturally dominate revenue attribution. The doughnut skew is not a signal that other channels are failing; it reflects baseline traffic distribution. The more diagnostic metric is revenue per session, which normalises for volume and reveals which channels are efficient at converting the traffic they receive. A small channel with high RPS is often a strong candidate for deliberate scaling.

Total revenue is a volume metric — it grows simply by sending more traffic regardless of quality. Revenue per session (RPS) is an efficiency metric: how much commercial value does the average visit from this channel generate? A channel with $0.80 RPS and 10,000 sessions will generate more incremental revenue when scaled than one with $0.10 RPS and 50,000 sessions. RPS is particularly powerful for budget allocation: if I pay to bring one more visitor from Channel A versus Channel B, which is worth more? It also accounts for average order value — a channel with high conversion rate but low AOV may still underperform on RPS.

Both. Direct in GA4 is assigned to sessions where no referrer is captured — bookmarks, typed URLs, native app links, or any channel where UTM parameters are missing or stripped. In practice, the Direct bucket mixes genuinely direct visits (brand recall, bookmarks) with misattributed traffic from untagged emails, dark social (WhatsApp, Slack, private messaging), and PDF or offline links. 83,677 direct sessions is plausible for the Google Merchandise Store given its brand recognition, but a portion will be misattributed email and social traffic. Interpret Direct performance conservatively and prioritise UTM tagging in email sends to reclaim that attribution clarity.

The channel table shows aggregate totals — how each channel performed overall. The area chart shows the temporal shape of that performance: whether channels rise and fall together (shared demand driver like seasonality), whether one accelerates while others plateau (campaign launch or algorithm shift), or whether the channel mix evolves over time. In this dataset, all channels spike in the Black Friday window — correlated demand. The diagnostic question is whether the relative channel contribution changes post-holiday. A shrinking CPC share after Week 8 would confirm paid spend was correctly wound down as demand cooled.

In this dashboard, conversion rate = purchase events ÷ reconstructed sessions per channel. This is a custom metric. GA4's own conversion rate uses key events and can apply user-level or session-scoped denominators depending on settings. Here, sessions are rebuilt from the BigQuery event export using CONCAT(user_pseudo_id, ga_session_id) as the session key, and purchases are isolated from event_name = 'purchase'. The result is a clean, channel-comparable funnel metric — but it will differ from the GA4 UI figure. Full methodology is documented on the dataset build page.

Because the dataset spans three distinct behavioural periods and channels perform differently across them. The last 30 days fall in the Cool-Down phase (January 2021), where overall purchase volume is recovering but still well below the November peak. Channels that depend on transactional urgency — especially CPC, which benefits from high-intent holiday queries — appear weaker in this window. Channels with compounding organic reach maintain more stable contributions. This is why evaluating channels across a full seasonal cycle matters: a channel that looks weak in a 30-day cool-down snapshot may have been the highest-performing channel during peak.

Average order value (AOV = revenue ÷ purchases) in this dataset sits around $68–72 overall, consistent with the Google Merchandise Store's product range. AOV is a signal of audience intent depth: channels with higher AOV attract buyers who either buy more items or select higher-priced products. A channel with high conversion rate but low AOV may generate many small purchases — volume noise rather than value signal. Comparing AOV across channels in the Rev / Session column quickly identifies which channels are acquiring high-value buyers versus high-frequency low-value ones.

Yes — the dashboard is data-portable. It loads three CSV files from an assets/ folder: ga4_funnel_by_day_channel.csv (semicolon-delimited, DD/MM/YYYY dates), ga4_purchases_revenue_by_day.csv (comma-delimited, YYYY-MM-DD), and ga4_sessions_by_day_channel.csv (comma-delimited, YYYY-MM-DD). Replace these files with your own exports shaped to the same column headers and the dashboard — all charts, KPIs, insights, and the channel filter — repopulates automatically. The BigQuery SQL to generate these exact shapes from any GA4 export is on the dataset build page.

Not necessarily. The funnel CSV records purchase events only where they appeared in the GA4 export — sessions that didn't result in a purchase event on the same day show zero. Legitimate causes include browsing-only sessions, differences between GA4's session-scoped and event-scoped attribution models, and channels like (data deleted) / referral representing GDPR-anonymised records stripped of full attribution detail. Channels appearing as <Other> are catch-all buckets for traffic not matching GA4's standard channel groupings. These are data quality artefacts, not genuine zero-conversion channels.

ROAS = Revenue ÷ Ad Spend. The GA4 BigQuery export does not include cost data — spend lives in the Google Ads export. To compute ROAS you would join a Google Ads performance export (daily spend by campaign or ad group) against this revenue data on date and channel. If Google / CPC generated approximately $13,000 in attributed revenue and daily spend was ~$200/day over 91 days (~$18,200 total), ROAS would be roughly 0.71× — spend exceeding revenue, a strong signal to review bidding strategy or landing page quality. This limitation is explicitly documented: the next iteration of this project incorporates a paid media spend table to unlock CPA and ROAS analysis.
